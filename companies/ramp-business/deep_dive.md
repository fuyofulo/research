## 1. What Ramp Business actually is (and isn't)

Ramp Business Corporation — operating as **Ramp**, marketing site **ramp.com** — is a New York-headquartered B2B fintech that sells corporate charge cards, expense management, bill-pay/AP automation, procurement, business-travel booking, and a treasury/cash-management product to U.S. businesses. It explicitly positions itself as the "AI-native finance platform" and competes against Brex, Mercury (banking-adjacent), Navan (travel + cards), Coupa, BILL, Concur/Expensify, and ultimately the corporate-card divisions of American Express, JPMorgan, and Capital One ✅ ([Wikipedia: Ramp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramp_(company)); [Ramp Press](https://ramp.com/press)).

**Critical disambiguation**: this is *not* **Ramp Network / ramp.network**, the London-based fiat-to-crypto on-ramp. They share no founders, investors, or corporate relationship. ✅ This stream is exclusively about Ramp Business / ramp.com. (Confusion is common because both raised heavily in 2021–22.)

## 7. Regulatory licenses & compliance footprint

Ramp is **not a bank** and **does not hold its own card-issuing license**. The structure:


| Function                                                        | Partner                                                                                                                    | Confidence |
| --------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------- |
| Card-issuing bank — Ramp Visa **Commercial** Card (charge card) | **Sutton Bank** (Attica, OH; FDIC member)                                                                                  | ✅          |
| Card-issuing bank — Ramp Visa **Corporate** Card                | **Celtic Bank** (Salt Lake City; FDIC member; ILC)                                                                         | ✅          |
| Card processor (issuer-processor)                               | **Marqeta** (originally) and **Stripe Issuing** for parts of the program                                                   | ✅          |
| Card network                                                    | **Visa** (Sutton & Celtic both Visa BIN sponsors)                                                                          | ✅          |
| Bill Pay / ACH rails                                            | **Increase** (BaaS platform), which fronts FDIC-insured banks Grasshopper, First Internet Bank of Indiana, Twin City, Core | ✅          |
| Treasury — checking/operating account                           | **First Internet Bank of Indiana** (FDIC); FDIC sweep through **IntraFi ICS** for higher coverage                          | ✅          |
| Treasury — investment / money-market                            | **Apex Clearing** (SEC-registered broker-dealer; FINRA/SIPC)                                                               | ✅          |


**State Money Transmitter Licenses (MTLs)**: Because Bill Pay and Treasury involve moving money on behalf of customers, Ramp likely operates as an **agent of bank** under its banking partnerships rather than holding broad state MTLs. The architecture (Bill Pay routes through Increase, Treasury sits under First Internet Bank of Indiana) strongly suggests Ramp uses the **agent-of-payee / agent-of-bank** exemption. 🟡

**What this means strategically**: Ramp has built a "thin" regulatory footprint — let banks be banks, take the spread/fee economics, avoid the capital-requirement and exam burden of being a regulated financial institution itself. The regulatory exposure is therefore *vendor-concentration* (Sutton, Celtic, First Internet Bank, Increase) rather than direct prudential. ✅

**No FinCEN consent order, OCC action, or CFPB enforcement** has surfaced against Ramp. ✅ clean public record.

## 9. The pivot — corporate card → spend management → finance automation → AI-native finance

This is one of the cleaner narrative arcs in fintech, and Ramp tells it deliberately:

**Phase 1 (2019–2020): "1.5% cashback corporate card."** Pure card with savings-oriented marketing — explicit positioning *against* Brex's points/rewards model. Insight from the Capital One years: incumbents profit from clients spending more; Ramp would profit from clients spending *less*. ✅

**Phase 2 (2021–2022): "Spend management platform."** Bill Pay launches in 2021, expense automation, accounting integrations, vendor management. The pitch becomes "card + software" — interchange revenue subsidizes rapid software-product expansion. Buyer acquisition (Aug 2021) is the first non-card capability. ✅

**Phase 3 (2022–2023): "Finance automation."** Procurement (Ramp Plus, Jul 2023) and the Cohere.io acquisition (Jun 2023) shift the language — Ramp is now selling "automation of the finance function," not just card+expense. ✅

**Phase 4 (May 2023 onward): "AI-powered finance platform."** Ramp Intelligence (May 2023) is the explicit pivot. By 2024, the marketing replaces "spend management" with "AI-powered finance platform." ✅

**Phase 5 (Jul 2025 onward): "AI-native finance" / "autonomous finance."** With the Series E ($16B) and especially Series E-2 ($22.5B), Ramp explicitly markets that the $500M Iconiq round is to "rush AI" — building agents, hiring AI engineers/product/sales focused on agentic offerings. The Nov 2025 round ($32B Lightspeed) uses the phrase "autonomous finance," and Glyman publicly stated "autonomous finance will arrive within three years." ✅

The verbal evolution is precise: **cashback → spend management → finance automation → AI-powered finance → AI-native / autonomous finance.** Each shift roughly aligns with a major funding round and a new product line.

## Summary observations for the AI × crypto research thesis

For your AI-x-crypto research, four Ramp-specific signals matter:

1. **Ramp is the most-funded "AI-native" software company that is *not* an LLM lab.** $2.3B+ raised explicitly to build agentic finance infrastructure. ✅
2. **Ramp owns a unique transactional dataset on AI vendor spend and AI agent activity.** Glyman has publicly disclosed Ramp can see foundation-model market share shift in real time (49% → 9% in 10 months, Oct 2024). This is the closest thing to a "Bloomberg terminal" for AI consumption that exists. 🟡
3. **The "autonomous finance" thesis directly maps onto agentic crypto payments.** If AI agents will execute B2B payments autonomously by 2027 (Glyman's claim), the payment rail underneath them is contested — and stablecoins/crypto rails are a credible substitute for ACH/card. Ramp has not publicly committed to crypto rails, but their Increase + Stripe stack already supports adjacent infrastructure. 🟡
4. **Ramp's clean regulatory posture (no MTLs of its own; agent-of-bank model) is replicable for AI-native crypto fintechs.** Anyone building "Ramp for AI agents on crypto rails" can copy the partner-bank architecture. ✅
