# Blockworks — Deep Dive (April 2026)

> Crypto media + research + events business. Founded 2017–18 by Jason Yanowitz and Michael Ippolito. Killed their news division in October 2025 and pivoted hard to a data-and-distribution model.
> **Date:** 2026-04-29
> **Sources:** Web research agent + 3 founder podcast transcripts (MFM Pod 2023 ~$25M revenue interview, Hidden Forces Jan 2021 pivot/origin, Media Empires Feb 2023 trust + revenue mix). Transcripts saved in `transcripts/`.

---

## TL;DR

Blockworks is the **most operationally successful crypto media company of the cycle**. Bootstrapped from 2017 with $100K friends-and-family until May 2023, profitable every year but one, projected **>$30M revenue in 2025** with claimed $0 marketing spend ("reverse CAC"). Single $12M Series A at $135M post-money (10T Holdings + Framework + Santiago Santos angel) is the only meaningful equity dilution in the company's history.

**Three things the agent surfaced that re-anchor everything else:**

1. **They shut down their news division on October 29, 2025.** Laid off Katherine Ross, Casey Wagner, Ben Strack, others. Ended Blockworks-as-media-company. New positioning: data + distribution. ([Blockhead](https://www.blockhead.co/2025/10/30/blockworks-shuts-down-news-division-lays-off-journalists-in-pivot-to-data-business/))
2. **Yanowitz was at Sisense pre-Blockworks** — a $300M-raised data-analytics startup. The pivot to a data product is *founder going home*, not opportunism.
3. **They were bootstrapped for 6 years.** $0 outside capital from 2017 → May 2023. Built distribution before product.

**Three things the founder transcripts add that the agent didn't surface as cleanly:**

4. **Yanowitz's pre-college origin is a ~4,600-person downline in a multi-level-marketing energy-drink scheme** — direct from his mouth on MFM. He realized it was Herbalife-adjacent only after watching the Bill Ackman / Carl Icahn battle. Got pulled out by his junior-year roommate. *That's where his sales/distribution muscles came from.*
5. **Permissionless single-event revenue in 2023 was >$10M.** Direct from Yanowitz: *"Last year we did well over $10 million from Permissionless."* Single conference, one weekend.
6. **Research v1 cost ~$1M to build.** Team of 8 analysts + a couple of engineers. Started at $25/month. *"Politico Pro sold for a billion. Bloomberg does $9B in subscription revenue. Refinitiv sold for $27B."* — Yanowitz on what he's gunning for.

**The thesis you should walk away with:** Blockworks is no longer a media company. It's a **distribution-flywheel business** that is now monetizing the same audience seven ways (events, sponsorships, paid stage, newsletters, podcasts, research subs, data API) and adding an institutional B2B-SaaS layer (Lightspeed IR, Solana Foundation partnership, Dec 2025) on top. The "Bloomberg of crypto" framing is louder than substantive in 2026, but **the data line is fastest-growing, has the highest-value dollar, and is run by a founder whose pre-Blockworks job was data SaaS at Sisense**. That's not nothing.

## 2. Founders & origin

### Jason Yanowitz — co-founder / CEO

**Background:**
- Bitcoin discovery: Hungary, 2015. Hungarian students into Bitcoin "as self-sovereign money" (his framing — government inflation had wiped families' savings)
- Pre-Blockworks at **Sisense** ($300M data-analytics startup) doing BD, then Intuitive Venture Partners
- "Got laughed out of the room" pitching crypto to TradFi VCs — frustration is the wedge

**The MLM origin (founder-direct, MFM Pod, late 2023):**

> *"Senior year of high school I wanted some money. Mom said go work at Taco Bell with Jake. I said I'm not working at Taco Bell."*

Yanowitz's college side hustle was a multi-level-marketing energy-drink scheme. By his sophomore year at Emory:
- Downline of **~4,600 people** (later ~600 on the other side)
- Made ~$1,500/week as a sophomore
- Got a **BMW** from the company
- Pitched the scheme on Skype to "rooms of 500 people"
- Realized it was Herbalife-adjacent only after watching Bill Ackman vs. Carl Icahn live
- His junior-year roommate slapped him out: *"All right man, we're gonna get internships, you should join us, stop doing this."*
- The CEO of the company eventually went to jail for 1–2 years

Why this matters: Yanowitz himself credits this experience for two things: (a) the LinkedIn cold-messaging hustle that bootstrapped Blockworks (*"Block work started by just me posting on LinkedIn a lot about crypto"*) and (b) the confidence to start a company from nothing. Without naming this, every founder story he tells about the early days starts from these patterns. **The founder you're evaluating learned distribution at scale at age 19 in a structurally questionable model and applied it to a clean industry.**

### Michael Ippolito — co-founder / COO/CFO-equivalent

- Pre-Blockworks at **Applied Value**, a boutique strategy consultancy. Advised Fortune 500 manufacturers on cost, capital efficiency, M&A
- The user's question about "Civic Capital" as Mike's prior job appears to be a misremembering — no such affiliation surfaces. Likely confused with Applied Value.
- Today: runs content + finance (he hosts On the Margin podcast)

### How they met / how Blockworks started (combined from MFM + Hidden Forces)

- Roommates in NYC in 2017
- One Sunday in late 2017, Jason went to a [brunchwork](https://brunchwork.com/) event in NYC arriving an hour early. Sat through an Ethereum talk by **Amanda Gutterman** (then CMO at ConsenSys) before the side-hustle / consulting talk he came for
- Came home that afternoon, "burst in the door" and told Mike: *"I'm launching a consulting firm to help companies figure out Ethereum"* — hours after first learning about it
- Mike's reaction (he was at Applied Value at the time, knew consulting): *"This doesn't sound like that good of an idea... it's really difficult to sell consulting if you don't have brand and reputation"*
- Counter-proposal: *"What if we hosted events? We went to that event last month that made some money."*
- **Six years later: zero consulting clients, but a media company**
- First event: **February 2018, 220 people, $12K top-line revenue**

The "$50K event" realization moment that hooked them, from Hidden Forces (Jan 2021):

> *"We paid like a couple hundred bucks each to go sit and listen to these people on stage. We were so pissed off. But then we looked around — 200–300 other people in this room, all paid the same amount, plus a couple sponsors. We were like — this event made $50 grand."*

**Founding date ambiguity:** Yanowitz publicly says *"profitable every year but one since 2017 founding"* (Fortune Apr 2025). The Our Journey page and Ippolito's anniversary post both say 2018. Best read: incorporated late 2017, materially operating 2018.

### Team size trajectory

- 2019: ~10–15
- 2021–22: hiring spree as podcasts + news scaled
- Late 2023 (founder-stated on MFM): **~65 employees**
- Apr 2025 (Fortune, founder-stated): **85 employees**, 15 in editorial

NYC HQ. Remote-friendly. No major secondary HQ.

## 5. Business model & unit economics

### The "reverse CAC" claim

Yanowitz's recurring framing: **$0 marketing spend.** Events generate cash and audience → audience feeds podcasts → podcasts feed newsletters → newsletters feed research subs → research subs feed events. A flywheel where each layer pays for itself and feeds the next. ([Fortune](https://fortune.com/crypto/2025/04/08/blockworks-yanowitz-crypto-data/))

If true (and the bootstrapped 2017–2023 history strongly suggests it is), gross margins on incremental subs are extreme.

### Revenue trajectory (founder-stated)

| Year | Revenue | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 2018 | First event $12K topline (220 people) | MFM (Yanowitz) |
| 2019 | Mostly events (~82% of revenue) | A Media Operator |
| 2020 | Events revenue down 80% (COVID) | Hidden Forces (Yanowitz) |
| 2022 | "Goal of $20M, exceeded by decent chunk" → ~$22–25M | Media Empires (Yanowitz, Feb 2023) |
| 2023 | Sam Parr's research (on MFM) had $25M revenue / $7–8M EBITDA — Yanowitz neither confirmed nor denied | MFM (late 2023) |
| 2024 | (estimate) ~$25–28M | Inferred — ad market down per Yanowitz |
| 2025 | **>$30M projected** | Fortune (Apr 2025, Yanowitz quote) |

### The "every dollar of revenue isn't equal" framing

Yanowitz on Media Empires (Feb 2023):
> *"Conferences make a really large amount of money and have really good margins. But a dollar of revenue is not the same across the whole business — that's almost the least valuable dollar. The more valuable dollars are podcasts and newsletters and the digital side. And one of the big focuses right now is scaling research, which is the recurring subscription side — the hardest revenue to get but the most valuable dollar."*

This is the strategic logic behind the Oct 2025 pivot: kill the lowest-margin / least-defensible line (free-news editorial), double down on subscription + data + B2B SaaS.

### What he's optimizing for personally

Yanowitz on MFM:
> *"Mike and I don't pay ourselves that much. Most of my money is just in Blockworks."*
> *"I was 100% in crypto until very recently."*
> *"I'm optimizing for fulfillment, autonomy, and personal growth — not to be a billionaire-style entrepreneur."*

## 6. Conflicts & editorial integrity (the candid section)

Multiple ongoing tensions. None disqualifying, but real:

1. **Pay-to-stage** — confirmed by Fortune. ~$20K for a panel, ~$100K+ for a fireside. Yanowitz defends with "max 20% of programming."
2. **Podcast editing for guests** — per a former employee in Fortune, podcast guests have been allowed to remove portions before release. Yanowitz says it has happened ~5 times, only for financial firms that wouldn't otherwise come on. The former employee called this "PR more than journalism." Both can be true.
3. **Empire co-host is a shareholder.** Santiago Santos invested in the Series A and co-hosts Empire with Yanowitz. Yanowitz personally invests in projects covered on Empire (DAWN, Solana-ecosystem deals). Disclosed but structural.
4. **Solana coloration.** The Solana Foundation is a **partner in Lightspeed IR** (Dec 2025 launch). The flagship Solana podcast is also called Lightspeed. Permissionless has been Solana-coded. Whether Yanowitz has a formal Solana Foundation advisory seat I could not verify in public sources — but the *commercial pattern* (ecosystem partner, podcast namesake, Permissionless lineup) is a soft conflict regardless.
5. **FTX / SBF aftermath.** No evidence Blockworks took FTX/Alameda money the way **The Block did** (CEO Mike McCaffrey resigned in Dec 2022 over a secret $43M Alameda loan to him personally). Permissionless I (May 2022) had FTX-adjacent presence (Multicoin, Solana-aligned VCs), but Blockworks emerged from FTX's collapse without a corresponding scandal. **This is arguably the single biggest reason Blockworks pulled ahead of The Block.**
6. **The October 2025 shutdown removed one side of the editorial-vs-business firewall** rather than reinforcing it. Yanowitz's pre-shutdown defense was "15 editorial in 85 staff, founders don't interfere day-to-day." Post-shutdown, that defense is no longer required.

## 9. Threats / red flags

1. **Crypto-cycle dependence on events.** Events still ~40% of revenue. A sharp 2026–27 bear market evaporates sponsor budgets. Survived 2022–23 once but the data line isn't yet big enough to be the counter-cyclical anchor.
2. **News-shutdown reputational backlash.** Crypto Twitter noticed. Some institutional clients chose Blockworks specifically for editorial credibility — that selling point is gone.
3. **Founder/business conflict surface area.** Yanowitz's personal positions, the Solana Foundation partnership, the Empire co-host shareholder situation — all exploitable by competitors who want a narrative.
4. **The "Bloomberg of crypto" race is crowded.** Messari, Kaiko, Coin Metrics, Glassnode, Artemis, Token Terminal, Dune, plus institutional sell-side. Blockworks's edge is distribution; not historically the strongest pure data shop.
5. **Solana exposure.** Lightspeed IR is Solana-only at launch. If Solana underperforms in 2026, the IR product is concentrated.
6. **Founder fatigue risk.** Yanowitz has been running the business + the most popular flagship podcast (Empire) for 8+ years.
7. **Catch-up moves from CoinDesk (Bullish capital) + Bankless (DAO).** The shutdown of Blockworks news created an opening.

## 10. What surprised me (combining agent + transcripts)

1. **Yanowitz's MLM origin is the single most explanatory fact about him.** A 19-year-old who built a 4,600-person downline learning to pitch on Skype to 500 people at a time becomes a 28-year-old who can bootstrap a $30M media company by sending 300 LinkedIn DMs a day. **It's the same skill.** Not flattering on its face, but it's the deep cause of Blockworks's distribution moat.

2. **Sisense, not WSJ, is the right Yanowitz-pre-Blockworks comp.** A $300M data-analytics company, not a media company. The Bloomberg-of-crypto pivot is the founder going home, not opportunism.

3. **Single-event revenue >$10M for Permissionless 2023.** That's larger than most crypto-media companies' total annual revenue.

4. **Bootstrapped for SIX YEARS.** $0 outside capital from 2017 → May 2023. Most crypto founders raise on Day 0. Blockworks built distribution first, then took capital — and kept it small ($12M, single round).

5. **They have $0 marketing spend.** Yanowitz claims "reverse CAC" — every product line refeeds the next. If true (and the math from a profitable bootstrapped 6-year run says it probably is), the unit economics are almost unbeatable.

6. **The Oct 2025 news shutdown happened in a record revenue year.** That's a "we have nothing to apologize for" move, not a "we failed" move. Yanowitz killed editorial because it wasn't valuable enough to keep, not because it was losing money. **That's a different — and more aggressive — kind of operator.**

7. **Lightspeed IR is the most underdiscussed product they have.** A genuine institutional infrastructure play (issuer-allocator pipes), launched in December 2025 in partnership with the Solana Foundation, easy to miss. If it works it's a real wedge into a category (crypto investor relations) that doesn't really exist yet.

8. **The first Blockworks podcast wasn't theirs — it was Pomp's.** Yanowitz produced/edited/sold sponsorships for Anthony Pompliano's "Off the Chain" (now "The Pomp Podcast"). They learned the podcast business as an agency before building their own IP. *"I'd go into Pomp's office seven days a week, Saturdays and Sundays. I learned GarageBand. I sold sponsorships."* They later realized agency-mode was great for revenue, terrible for IP, and built their own podcast network on top.

9. **Yanowitz himself does not want to be Brett Adcock.** From MFM: *"I don't want to spend my time doing things I should do. I would rather read a book and be by myself on a Sunday than mess with robots in a warehouse."* This is a *pragmatic operator with an exit number*, not a moonshot founder. **That's both a strength (the company will keep optimizing for margin) and a structural ceiling (no Empire-of-AI vision).**

10. **Sam Parr's 2023 prediction:** *"Yanowitz will have a multi-hundred-million-dollar exit before age 33, very likely worth $100M liquid in 5 years."* Worth tracking. Parr is connected.

11. **The pre-Blockworks consulting plan that pivoted to events that pivoted to media that pivoted to data:** *"We started as a conference business to sell consulting. Six years later we have no consulting."* The whole company is a chain of pivots away from the original idea — and that's the *correct* lesson, not a failure. Yanowitz's framing on MFM: *"99% of first-time founders did not have a Grand Vision. You take one foot in front of the other day after day and slog it out."*

12. **Their pivot sequence is the canonical bootstrap-to-data playbook.** Events → agency podcasts → owned podcasts → newsletters → editorial → research → data → IR SaaS. Each step was crisis-driven (COVID killed events, ad market killed news). **Each layer became higher-margin and more defensible than the last.** This is the textbook.

## 11. Why this matters for the AI × crypto research thesis

Blockworks is not a primary problem-finding target the way Credible was. It's instead three lateral useful things for the user's research thesis:

1. **Their AI × crypto research output is one of the more credible institutional viewpoints.** Worth reading the Blockworks Research thesis on AI agents to triangulate against your own. ([AI Agent Platforms Survey](https://app.blockworksresearch.com/unlocked/ai-agent-platforms-a-survey))

2. **Lightspeed IR is itself an AI-adjacent problem space.** Investor relations workflows are exactly the kind of high-context, high-stakes coordination problem where AI agents, structured data pipes, and human-in-the-loop trust will collide first. Watch how it evolves — if Blockworks bolts AI agents into the IC-memo / governance-feed / allocator-comms loop, that becomes a template for "AI agents inside regulated B2B workflows" that's far ahead of where most agent-payments companies are.

3. **The bootstrap-to-data playbook is replicable.** If you're going to start an AI × crypto media or research business — or even an AI-agent-discovery-and-distribution business — Blockworks's six-year bootstrap into a $30M flywheel is the model. Events → owned content → research subs → data → SaaS infrastructure. Not VC-burnt, not token-funded, profitable from year one.
