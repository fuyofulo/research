## 2. The SVB inflow event (March 2023) — $2B in 5 days

This is the canonical Mercury growth story and is unusually well-documented.

**Timeline and magnitudes (✅ multiple sources: Fortune, TechCrunch, Banking Dive, American Banker, Sacra):**

- **Mar 9, 2023** — Immad Akhund notices SVB rumor traffic; signup volume picks up.
- **Mar 10-11** — SVB shuttered by FDIC. Mercury launches **Mercury Vault** *"over a weekend,"* extending FDIC coverage from $1M to $3M, then to $5M via partner-bank sweep networks (Choice + Evolve).
- **5 days post-collapse**: 8,700+ new customers, $2B+ in fresh deposits — the highest among competing digital-banking platforms.
- **April–June 2023**: 17,000 additional new customers with deposits.
- **4 months post-collapse**: ~26,000 net-new customers (TechCrunch, Jul-2023).
- **Retention**: Mercury claimed **95%** of net-new customers stuck around at the 90-day mark; deposits held steady. ✅ (Fortune May-2023, repeated by Akhund publicly).
- **Revenue impact**: Annualized run-rate grew **4x YoY** May-2022 → May-2023.
- **Market-share claim**: 20% of SVB customers reportedly opened a Mercury account post-collapse (analyst estimate, not Mercury-confirmed). 🟡

**Did Mercury keep these customers?** Yes — the inflow proved sticky enough to power Mercury's Series C and the 200K → 300K customer ramp through 2024-2025. The clearest evidence is that Mercury's 2025 annualized revenue ($650M) is roughly 4-5x what it was when the SVB cohort arrived, suggesting either retention or aggressive cross-sell or both. ✅

**Capacity crunch in real time:** Sifted, Fortune, and Banking Dive all describe Mercury hitting hard onboarding bottlenecks during the SVB week. One founder testimonial reproduced on Hoop.app describes opening a Mercury account *at 4:52pm EST and wiring funds by 9pm* — fast enough to clear payroll, but only by a hair.

**Akhund's retention playbook (Fortune Q&A):**
1. Raise FDIC coverage from $1M to $5M.
2. Layer in T-bill money-market funds (Mercury Vault / Treasury) — *"either go to a too-big-to-fail or go to somewhere like Mercury where you can get a larger set of FDIC insurance."*
3. UX upgrades aimed at VCs (one email across multiple orgs).

This event is also what caused Mercury's later regulatory pain. The 26K-customer surge included a large number of *non-US founders* whose underlying KYC was thin — which directly fed into the Choice Bank / Evolve FDIC consent-order saga in 2024.

## 3. Synapse migration & Evolve exit (2023-2024)

Mercury's positioning during the Synapse collapse is genuinely useful to understand because *Mercury was both partially exposed and partially insulated*, which is unusual.

**The structural picture:**

- Mercury launched in 2019 as a UI layer on top of Synapse + Lithic + Choice Bank.
- **Sept 28 – Oct 10, 2023**: Mercury performed a fund migration off Synapse, moving end-user balances directly to Evolve Bank & Trust (cutting out Synapse as middleman). ✅
- **April 2024**: Synapse files for Chapter 11. >$200M of end-user funds are frozen across Synapse's partners (Yotta, Juno, Copper) — Mercury customers were *not* among the frozen accounts because Mercury had already migrated off in late 2023.
- **May 2024**: Synapse founder Sankaet Pathak alleges in court filings that Mercury / Evolve over-migrated ~$50M during the transition. Synapse countersues Mercury for **$36M+**.
- **June-July 2024**: Mercury publicly denies impropriety — *"Mercury did not over-migrate any funds during the transition... despite the lack of evidence provided by the former Synapse CEO, Mercury investigated the claim fully and found no evidence to support it."* ✅
- **Later 2024**: Mercury announces it is **cutting ties with Evolve Bank & Trust** and consolidating onto Choice Financial Group (and Column N.A. for some products), partly because Evolve was itself under increasing OCC / FDIC scrutiny.

**Customer impact comparison:**

| Fintech | Customer outcome from Synapse collapse |
|---|---|
| Yotta | Catastrophic — $112M+ in customer funds locked, many partially unrecoverable for months. |
| Juno | Severe disruption, account access frozen. |
| Copper (kid-banking) | Shut down product completely. |
| **Mercury** | **Effectively unaffected** — pre-migrated off Synapse. Public messaging emphasized this. |

Mercury's Synapse handling is, on net, a customer-trust win that Mercury has not bragged about loudly because (a) it didn't want to draw attention to having ever been on Synapse and (b) the FDIC/Choice fallout overshadowed the narrative anyway.

**Sankaet Pathak's Medium counter-narrative**: Pathak published a piece titled "Mercury Reconciliation Issues" on Medium and elsewhere accusing Mercury of moving more user funds than entitled. American Banker, Fintech Takes (Jason Mikula), and Fintech Business Weekly have all parsed this dispute. Verdict in fintech-press consensus: **Mercury's account is the more credible one**, but the dispute is unresolved and Mercury's behaviour around the "whitelist" of high-risk customers Synapse alleges Mercury pushed Evolve to onboard is a soft spot. 🟡

## 4. Crypto-founder closures (2021-2023) — softened?

Mercury has a long-standing reputation for being unfriendly to crypto operators, *despite* showcasing Phantom as a marquee customer.

**Early reputation (2021-2022):**

- Reddit/Hacker News/OffshoreCorpTalk threads from 2021-2023 document repeated accounts of crypto-adjacent businesses (NFT minters, OTC desks, mixers, market-makers, even Solana protocol DAOs) being closed without notice.
- Mercury's TOS at the time prohibited:
  - Crypto exchanges, custodial wallets, mining operations.
  - "Money services business" activity broadly.
  - Frequent stablecoin on/off-ramp activity using their account.

**Post-2023 evolution:**

- Mercury **launched `mercury.com/web3`** explicitly courting crypto founders, with Phantom as headline reference.
- Akhund has personally been a frequent angel investor in crypto/web3 startups, increasing public alignment.
- However, in the **2024 mass account closures** (see §5), crypto-related businesses were swept up alongside the geographic closures, because many had complex international cap tables.

**Has Mercury softened in 2025-2026?** Partially:
- Yes for clean US-LLC crypto teams (wallets, infrastructure, dev tooling).
- Still hostile to anything resembling MSB activity (exchanges, OTC, mixers, custodial services for retail).
- Still cautious about high-risk-jurisdiction crypto teams.

So the post-2023 "softening" is real but narrow. The platform's stated and revealed preference is: *we'll bank crypto-product companies (Phantom, OpenSea-style) but not crypto-financial-service companies (exchanges, OTCs, custodians)*. 🟡

## 5. Regulatory storm: Choice Bank consent order + 2024 mass closures

**The Information's investigation (May 2024)** — *"Inside Mercury's Stumble From Fintech Hero to Target of the Feds"* — is the canonical primary source, not the NYT. (The user's mention of "NYT investigation" appears to conflate The Information's reporting; **I found no NYT investigation matching the description**. The Information broke the federal-scrutiny angle on Mercury, and Banking Dive / TechCrunch / Sifted picked it up.) 🔴 on the NYT attribution.

**FDIC Choice Financial Group consent order — Jan 2024:**

- Joint exam by the FDIC and the North Dakota Department of Financial Institutions found Choice in violation of the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) and FDIC implementing regulations.
- Order required improved board oversight of BSA/AML, third-party (fintech partner) compliance, and an improved Customer Identification Program (CIP) / Customer Due Diligence (CDD) regime.
- Choice was specifically cited for letting overseas Mercury customers *"open thousands of accounts using questionable methods to prove they had a presence in the U.S."* — i.e. the Stripe Atlas / no-real-US-nexus problem.
- The FDIC was *"concerned"* that Choice *"had opened Mercury accounts in legally risky countries."*

**What had been happening (the Information's reconstruction):**

- Mercury, post-SVB inflow, took on large numbers of accounts from founders in geographies regulators considered high-risk: Ukraine, Nigeria, Pakistan, Venezuela, Haiti, Croatia, Philippines, and (apparently in some cases) Russia and Iran.
- Mercury pushed Synapse, and later Evolve, to "whitelist" certain such users.
- The FDIC viewed this as a third-party risk transmission problem — Choice was effectively renting its charter for KYC processes it didn't directly control.

**Customer wave — July 22-23, 2024:**

- Mercury sent termination emails giving affected customers ~3 weeks (until ~August 13, 2024) to wind down.
- **Affected geographies** (per Mercury, per Sifted/TechCrunch/Techpoint/AIN/Tech in Africa/Odessa Journal):
  - Ukraine, Nigeria, North Korea, Iran, Libya, Russia (sanctions-driven, fully expected).
  - **Also**: 13 African countries including Zimbabwe and Mali; Venezuela; Croatia; Philippines; Pakistan; Haiti — i.e., not all under US sanctions, but flagged as high-risk by Mercury post-Choice order.
- **Specific founder stories** documented:
  - **A16z-backed European startup paying Ukrainian engineers** — account closed (Sifted's lead anecdote).
  - **Nigerian YC alums (Techstars / YC accelerator grads)** — accounts of >30 startups restricted as early as Mar-2022, with the 2024 wave being broader.
  - **Ukrainian founders living physically in Ukraine but operating Delaware C-corps**: explicitly told *"too complex"* to support given current sanctions architecture.

**The brand damage:** Severe, but contained. The non-US-founder community (which Mercury had specifically courted via Stripe Atlas — see §6.1) felt betrayed. Coverage in Sifted, Kyiv Independent, Tech in Africa, TechCabal, BitKE, Techpoint Africa, and growthhq.io was uniformly negative. Mercury's defense was the consent-order framework — *"we can't responsibly continue, this is a regulator-driven decision."* That defense was technically correct but, in the founder community, didn't restore trust.

## 6. Six detailed customer walkthroughs

### 6.1 The International Founder (Stripe Atlas → Mercury) — canonical, pre-2024

**Profile:** Non-US founder (typically India, Pakistan, Nigeria, Eastern Europe, LatAm). Uses Stripe Atlas to incorporate a Delaware C-Corp for $500, gets EIN, then opens a Mercury account *before EIN issuance* (the 2024 Stripe-Mercury partnership specifically enables this).

**Why Mercury was the default:**
- Could open a US business account *without* physically traveling to the US.
- No minimum deposit, no monthly fee.
- Free USD wires in/out — critical for paying overseas contractors and getting paid by US clients.
- Mercury's onboarding was 10-20 minutes (vs. 4-12 weeks for Chase / SVB international desk).

**The Stripe Atlas → Mercury funnel:** Mercury's partnership with Stripe Atlas (`mercury.com/blog/inside-mercury/mercury-stripe-atlas-partnership`) ✅ lets Atlas-incorporated companies *bypass the EIN waiting period*, opening banking even before the IRS issues the tax ID. Stripe Atlas runs across 180+ countries. Mercury captured a massive share of this funnel between 2019 and 2023.

**The 2024 break:** For many of these founders (specifically those with Ukrainian, Nigerian, Pakistani, Venezuelan addresses), the path closed in July 2024. Many migrated to **Wise Business** (limited), **Relay** (often the recommended successor for non-US founders), **Brex** (but Brex has its own bars), or **Mercury via a US co-founder** (workaround). ✅

**Notable founder voice (anonymized in Sifted reporting):** A Ukrainian founder with a16z-backed US company paying engineers via Mercury said the abrupt closure was *"emblematic of a problem in the whole banking system where banks don't differentiate Ukraine from Russia."*

### 6.2 The YC Startup ($500K SAFE deployment)

**Profile:** YC Summer 2024 batch company. Receives ~$500K in SAFE money (YC's $125K + Demo Day SAFEs from a dozen angels and microfunds). >50% of YC companies bank at Mercury. ✅

**Mercury usage pattern:**
- Operating account at Mercury (no fees, no minimums).
- Mercury IO credit card for SaaS / cloud / GPU spend with 1.5% cashback, no personal guarantee.
- ~$400K parked in Mercury Treasury earning ~4.0-4.5% net yield as of 2025 (Mercury claims up to 3.66% net for sub-$20M balances, up to ~4.47% in higher tiers per `mercury.com/treasury`).
- Bill Pay for contractor invoices.
- Multi-user access (founder + COO + accountant), with role-based permissions.

**Why not Brex/Ramp:** Brex (since Capital One acquisition April-2026, $5.15B) is increasingly perceived as having moved upmarket; Ramp is positioned as expense management not banking. Mercury still owns the *"primary operating account for a YC seed company"* slot.

### 6.3 The Series A Treasury Use Case ($5M raise)

**Canonical case study: Hathora.** ✅

- Multiplayer-game serverless infrastructure, raised Series A.
- *"The bulk of their funds — over 90% — are in Mercury Treasury, balancing security with growth."*
- Mercury Treasury **extended Hathora's runway by three months with minimal effort** (Mercury's own framing, Mercury blog).
- CEO Siddharth Dhulipalla quoted by name.

**The pattern:** Series A and B startups raising $5M–$50M use Mercury Treasury to sweep idle cash into a mix of:
- T-bill money-market funds (Vanguard VUSXX / Morgan Stanley funds).
- Apex Clearing-managed brokerage account.
- Sweep network for $5M+ FDIC.

Yield as of 2025: 4.0–4.5% net. Eligibility: $500K+ in deposits. Yield approaches 4.47% at the $20M+ tier.

The non-Hathora analog: **Bench (the bookkeeping startup, pre-collapse) and Vise and many YC Series A companies** are believed to have parked treasury in Mercury post-SVB, though Hathora is the cleanest publicly-named example.

### 6.4 E-commerce / Inventory Financing — CAPNOS

**Profile: CAPNOS** (flavored-pressurized-air inhalers, vaping-replacement). ✅

- Founder/CEO: Brendan Wang (named publicly on Mercury blog).
- On **third financing term** with Mercury Working Capital.
- Funds deployed across: inventory purchases, R&D, *and equity adjustments* (this last item being slightly unusual for working-capital — suggests Mercury is flexible).
- Mercury Working Capital terms: **flat-fee, fixed weekly repayments, no personal guarantee, no collateral, no acceleration on outperformance, fee discount on early repayment**.
- Eligibility: $250K annual sales + 6 months operating history.
- Application: 10-minute submission, 2-3 business-day underwriting response.

**The analog: Get Maine Lobster.** ✅ Mark Murrell (CEO) uses Mercury as the *operating-system layer* (bank + cards + Treasury + invoicing) to run a DTC seafood logistics business. Not the Working Capital product specifically but the full DTC e-comm bundle.

**Why it matters competitively:** This pushes Mercury into territory historically owned by Shopify Capital, Wayflyer, Clearco, and Parker. Mercury's pitch: *we already see your cash flow because we are your bank — so we underwrite faster and cheaper than people who don't.*

### 6.5 Mercury Personal alongside Mercury Business — the founder dual-stack

**Profile:** Tech founder with a $500K-$5M personal liquidity event (raised round, exit, secondary). Wants high-yield personal banking that doesn't suck and that integrates with the same dashboard as their company.

**Mercury Personal (launched April 17, 2024):** ✅
- **$240/year subscription.**
- 5.00% APY on high-yield savings at launch (now lower as Fed rates have eased through 2025-26).
- No-fee domestic wires and ACH.
- Customizable debit card limits.
- Worldwide ATM fee reimbursement.
- Multi-user (give spouse / family member access with custom permissions).
- Up to **$5M in FDIC** via sweep.
- **Premium Consumer Banking** product line launched **December 2025** specifically for "builders and founders" (Businesswire 11-Dec-2025).

**Beta-tester voice (named):** Shane Mac, multi-time founder, mind behind XMTP, said the product *"has surpassed my expectations"* and called it *"nothing short of revolutionary for consumer banking."* ✅

**Why it matters:** Mercury is one of *very few* US fintechs operating a unified founder + business banking stack. Competition: Robinhood Gold (much wider, less founder-tailored), Wealthfront / Betterment (yield only), Chase Sapphire / Schwab Bank (incumbents, ugly).

### 6.6 Founder who switched FROM Brex/Ramp TO Mercury (or vice versa)

**The Brex → Mercury direction** has been the dominant flow since 2022:
- Brex shed its small-startup customer base in mid-2022 (Brex's strategic "we no longer serve small businesses" pivot toward enterprise).
- Tens of thousands of pre-Series A startups were effectively pushed to Mercury and Rho.
- This was Mercury's largest organic-growth event *before* the SVB collapse — analyst writeups (Sacra, Contrary Research) date Mercury's true takeoff to Brex's small-startup exit.

**The Mercury → Ramp/Brex direction** is real but smaller:
- Ramp markets aggressively to Mercury customers tired of poor expense management. Ramp's `Brex vs Mercury vs Ramp` article and Piñata's published case study (`switched from Brex to Ramp, saved 20 hours/month, pushed receipt compliance to 95%`) ✅ illustrate Ramp's pull.
- Some Mercury customers move to **Rho** specifically for treasury + AP automation when they hit Series B.

**Capital One's $5.15B acquisition of Brex closed April 7, 2026.** ✅ For founders who originally chose Brex *as the modern alternative to big-bank infrastructure*, Brex is now part of the largest US card issuer. Anecdotally this is producing a small migration back to Mercury (or to Ramp), but the magnitude isn't yet quantified in public reporting.

6. **Card limits / debit limits** default low and require manual increase.
7. **Card cancellation during maintenance windows** has happened, surfacing in Trustpilot reviews.
8. **The "I had a horrible experience with Mercury bank"** HN thread (`news.ycombinator.com/item?id=28690963`) from a US citizen living abroad is the canonical sentiment piece — older, but still cited.

| Account-opening time | **10-20 min** | Days-weeks | Hours | Hours | Hours |

**The structural verdicts founders give in podcasts and reviewer surfaces:**

1. **Mercury wins as primary operating account** for any pre-Series A US startup, full stop.
2. **Ramp wins as the spend-management layer** if you're at 10+ employees with reimbursement pain.
3. **Brex wins at scale** if you're Series B+ with global ops, *though Capital One ownership has shifted that math*.
4. **Rho wins on integrated AP + treasury** for Series B with a finance team.
5. **Chase wins only as a backup** for FDIC redundancy.

## 9. Loss stories — what Mercury doesn't advertise

### 9.1 The 2024 non-US-founder purge

Already covered in §5. This is the single biggest customer-loss event in Mercury's history measured by *founder-trust damage* if not raw account count. Mercury has not published numbers on how many accounts were closed. The independent estimates (Sifted, growthhq.io, Tech in Africa) range from *several thousand* to *tens of thousands* of accounts.

### 9.2 The Synapse-Evolve transition (Sept-Oct 2023)

Mercury moved customers from Synapse-mediated FBO accounts at Evolve directly to Evolve, then later moved them again off Evolve. Customers experienced multiple letters and account-number changes. Largely went smoothly compared to the broader Synapse implosion, but *Mercury used to be on Synapse*, which Mercury would prefer customers not remember.

### 9.3 Products that quietly de-emphasized or shrank

- **International wires** — feature still exists, but per-country caps and outbound restrictions tightened materially during 2024.
- **Crypto-business onboarding** — opened in concept via `mercury.com/web3` but in practice gated on business type; many crypto applicants get bounced.
- **Mercury IO consumer credit** has had its underwriting tightened multiple times since 2022 launch; some founders had limits suddenly cut.
- **Mercury Raise as a cohort** — discontinued in favor of always-on platform model (this is positioned as expansion, but the cohort branding had real prestige that was lost).

### 9.4 Lost customer types that left

- **Crypto exchanges, OTCs, custodians** — never welcome to begin with; if they slipped in, were closed.
- **Cannabis-adjacent businesses** — never supported.
- **Gambling / igaming** — never supported.
- **High-volume e-commerce arbitrage / drop-shipping sellers** — closed in waves (eBay/Whatnot resellers featured in BBB complaints).
- **Adult content** — never supported.
- **Founders who moved to non-US residence** (even US citizens) — many closed.
- **Founders whose primary address became Ukraine / Nigeria / Pakistan / Venezuela / Croatia / Philippines / Haiti** in 2024.

### 9.5 The Brex / Ramp / Rho counter-pull

For Series B+ companies, Ramp's spend-management bite into Mercury is real but slow. Rho's full-stack-finance bite is also real for companies that want a single bank + AP + treasury platform. Mercury's response has been to deepen treasury, working capital, IO credit, and bill pay — but it has not yet pulled even with Ramp on spend-management UX.
